# Voice

How PrismaX sounds when it speaks. Pair this with [key-messages.md](key-messages.md) (what to say to whom) and [foundation.md](foundation.md) (what is true). This file is the *how*.

If a sentence reads like it could appear on any AI startup's homepage, rewrite it.

---

## Voice Principles

### 1. Quiet confidence
PrismaX has weight. It doesn't need to announce itself. Sentences land flat and true. No exclamation marks, no breathless framing, no "we're excited to share."

> The brand does not shout. It demonstrates.

### 2. Demonstrate, then explain
Show the operator at the console. Show the robot mid-task. Show the data trace. Then — if needed — explain what the reader is looking at. Visual proof carries more than a paragraph. Words exist to anchor what the image already said.

### 3. Editorial precision
Every sentence earns its length. Read it aloud. If it sounds like marketing, it is — rewrite. Prefer specific nouns and active verbs. Avoid stacked adjectives. One idea per sentence.

### 4. Builder-led, not pitch-led
Speak the way an engineer who built the system would describe it to a peer. Concrete. Technical when warranted. Honest about limits. Never oversold. This is the difference between a research lab's announcement and a startup's launch tweet — we sound like the former.

### 5. Infrastructure made legible
PrismaX is the invisible layer of robotics. Our writing makes the unseeable visible: the data pipeline, the training loop, the teleoperator's intent. Name the thing. Don't hide behind abstraction.

## How PrismaX Sounds — Sentence Patterns

| Pattern | Use |
|---|---|
| **"PrismaX is X."** | Declarative positioning. Verb is `is` or `operates`, not `believes` or `aims to`. |
| **"X enables Y."** | Causal, mechanical. Names the action, not the aspiration. |
| **"X — Y — Z."** | Em-dash clauses for editorial rhythm. Use sparingly; not every sentence. |
| **Lowercase technical terms.** | `physical AI`, not `Physical AI`. `human-in-the-loop`, not `Human-In-The-Loop`. |
| **Numbers when earned.** | Specific figures land. Vague ones ("thousands of hours," "global community") only when a specific number isn't yet public. |

## Internal Narrative Anchors

Three mental models the writer should hold open while drafting:

### The portal
PrismaX is the transformation layer between fragmented robotics today and capable autonomous systems tomorrow. Left of the mirror: dots, static, undefined. Right: solid, real, deployed. Every sentence should sit *in* the mirror — describing the act of crossing, not the static endpoints.

When you write "we help robotics companies deploy faster" — that's the right side, abstracted. When you write "PrismaX operates live systems where robotic work generates the data that physical AI advances on" — that's the mirror itself.

### Three pillars: Robots · Data · Intelligence
Everything PrismaX does maps to one of these. When in doubt, anchor a paragraph to a pillar. See [pillars.md](pillars.md).

### Service layer, not product
PrismaX is a layer the industry runs *on*, not a product the industry *buys*. Verbs reflect this: operate, deploy, standardize, enable. Not: sell, ship, launch.

## What to Never Write

### Hype vocabulary
- ❌ revolutionary, game-changing, cutting-edge, next-generation, world-class, best-in-class
- ❌ unlock, leverage, synergize, supercharge, accelerate (as a verb)
- ❌ AI-powered, AI-driven, AI-first, AI-native (when used as ornament rather than fact)
- ❌ transform (as a verb describing what PrismaX does to customers)

### Crypto-bro vocabulary
- ❌ LFG, gm, wagmi, ngmi, ser, anon, fren
- ❌ to the moon, 🚀, 🔥, 💎 — emoji-as-tone in general
- ❌ token-narrative framing of the product ("the future is decentralized robotics")
- ❌ "alpha," "early," "based" (as a tone marker)

### Generic launch/PR phrasing
- ❌ "We're excited to announce..."
- ❌ "Today, we're thrilled to share..."
- ❌ "Trusted by industry leaders" (name the leaders)
- ❌ "Join us on this journey"
- ❌ "Stay tuned for..."

### Vague credibility
- ❌ "Built by experts" → name the team
- ❌ "Backed by top investors" → name the investors
- ❌ "Industry-leading accuracy" → name the metric, the number, the comparison

## Voice by Audience Tier

Voice is consistent across tiers — but the *register* shifts. Match register to the reader.

| Tier | Register shift | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Tier 1 — Buyers** | Precise, integration-aware, ROI-honest. Names the metric. Names the cost. | "Cost per usable data hour. Time-to-dataset. Integration with your existing stack." |
| **Tier 2 — Researchers** | Technically honest, limits-forward, peer-paced. Cites. Acknowledges open questions. | "Operator-reported intent labels. Cross-operator agreement not yet measured. Dataset access on request." |
| **Tier 3 — Community** | Builder-energetic, visual-first, specific over slogan. Short sentences. Founder-voiced. | "12,000 sessions last week. 4 platforms. Watch the session log." |
| **Tier 4 — Amplifiers** | Category-clear, founder-positioned, "why now"-anchored. Quotable. | "The service layer for physical AI. Standards emerge from operation, not from spec sheets." |

The *core voice* — quiet confidence, demonstration over announcement, editorial precision — does not change. Only the technical density and the surface format shift.

## A Test Before You Ship

Before publishing any sentence:

1. **Read it aloud.** Does it sound like a person talking, or like a press release? If the latter, rewrite.
2. **Could a competitor write the same sentence?** If yes, it's too generic. Add specificity.
3. **Does it announce or demonstrate?** If it says "we are doing X," rewrite to show what X looks like.
4. **Is there a number?** If a number would land, find one. If a number doesn't yet exist for this claim, the claim itself may be the wrong claim.
5. **Did you use a word from the "Never Write" list?** Remove it. There is always a better word.
